## What the database shows

The market is already crowded with funded companies, lower-cost hardware, and products that have real deployments. What stood out to me was how hard it is for a buyer to compare cost and expected results. Only 3 of the 17 vendors publish a fixed price or an explicit subscription amount. The other 14 use quotes, enterprise agreements, or service models without stating the amount.

I see four gaps that InGen can test: clearer pilot economics, stronger proof of security outcomes, a multi-mission package for outdoor work, and a humanoid offer built around operating value instead of hardware price. The products involved are Sentinel Prime AI, Aido Rover, and Aido Humanoid. The figures below come from `Overview!B6:B12` and the three competitor tabs.

## Gap 1 — TCO and pilot-price transparency

Pricing is the clearest gap in the data. `Overview!B6:B8` shows that 3 of 17 vendors publish a fixed price, equal to 17.6%. All three are in the Humanoid tab. 1X lists a US$20,000 Early Access purchase and a later US$499 monthly plan (`Humanoid!E6:G6`). Unitree lists R1-D from US$4,290, R1 AIR at US$4,900, and the standard R1 at US$5,900 (`Humanoid!E8:G8`, Sources S40 and S42). Booster lists the K1 from US$5,999 (`Humanoid!E11:G11`, Source S36).

InGen could publish a standard 90-day pilot range and a short TCO worksheet, then customize the final proposal by site. For Sentinel Prime AI, the worksheet could show cost per monitored hour, manual patrol hours, monitoring support, and maintenance. For Aido Rover, it could show cost per completed patrol or inspection mission, charging, service, and deployment assumptions.

The worksheet does not need to predict every possible cost. It only needs to use the same basic categories for each pilot so a buyer can compare the proposal with current labor and service costs.

I would test the offer across at least 20 qualified opportunities. It should improve demo-to-pilot conversion by at least 15%, or shorten the median time from qualified demo to signed pilot by at least 20%. If neither happens, price was probably not the main reason customers hesitated.

## Gap 2 — Verified-event security workflow proof

The Security & Indoor tab contains five vendors, but only two are primarily security companies. `Overview!B9` and `Security & Indoor!M5:M9` identify Knightscope and Cobalt as purpose-built security vendors. KEENON, Bear Robotics, and Relay Robotics have commercial products, but their main work is delivery, cleaning, hospitality service, or material movement. This makes the security-first comparison narrower than the five-company tab first suggests.

Knightscope sells autonomous patrol through MaaS (`Security & Indoor!E5`). Cobalt reports that its AI Handlers investigate more than 80% of events, resolve up to 94% without human escalation, and respond in under 15 seconds on average (`Security & Indoor!J6`, Sources S03–S04). These figures show the kind of operating evidence a buyer may ask for.

Sentinel Prime AI should be measured in the same way. A pilot scorecard can track patrol coverage, validated alerts, non-actionable alerts, escalation time, unresolved priority incidents, and manual patrol hours. It should also show whether each alert was closed remotely or sent to site staff. Across 10 sites over 90 days, success means cutting manual patrol hours by at least 30% without increasing unresolved priority incidents. If incidents rise, the workflow is not ready to replace patrol time. If patrol hours do not fall, the value case should focus on coverage or risk reduction instead.

## Gap 3 — Outdoor multi-mission packaging

The outdoor market in this sample is mostly made up of focused tools. `Overview!B10` shows that 4 of 5 vendors in the Outdoor & Logistics tab are coded as a focused workflow or development platform. Avidbots focuses on cleaning, Locus on warehouse fulfillment, Effidence on material movement, and Clearpath on configurable robotics platforms. Boston Dynamics Spot is the only product in this set coded as broad inspection plus security. Its Spot/Orbit 5.1 update added a security-patrol mission alongside inspection functions (`Outdoor & Logistics!J6:M6`).

Aido Rover can compete by combining related jobs instead of trying to beat every specialist. One package could cover scheduled patrol, exception inspection, and light internal logistics under one service agreement. The main metric should be cost per completed mission.

A warehouse campus or industrial park would be a practical first setting because patrol, inspection, and small deliveries often use the same routes.

The test is whether at least 3 of the first 5 pilot customers activate two recurring workflows within 90 days and reduce cost per completed mission by at least 20% compared with separate tools or labor. If customers use only one workflow, InGen should sell a narrower package for that use case.

## Gap 4 — Humanoid value over hardware price

`Overview!B11:B12` gives a lowest public benchmark of US$4,290 and counts two vendor-level entry benchmarks below US$6,000: Unitree R1-D and Booster K1. Unitree's R1 AIR and standard R1 are also below US$6,000, but the formula counts one benchmark per vendor. R1-D is a contact-sales development configuration rather than a normal retail product (Source S42). Unitree also reports more than 5,500 humanoid robots sold and delivered in 2025 (`Humanoid!J8`, Source S31).

These public prices make a hardware-only value argument weaker. Aido Humanoid should focus on one workflow and show its task setup time, safety, uptime, integration effort, and support. A simple pilot could use one repetitive handling task and record how many engineering hours are needed before the robot performs it reliably.

For five controlled pilots, the target is at least 95% successful task completion and a 30% reduction in task-deployment time. The commercial test is whether customers then accept at least a 25% premium over a sub-US$6,000 developer platform. If those results are achieved but willingness to pay does not improve, the software and service layer is not different enough to support the premium.

## Recommended order

I would start with the pricing and security tests because they do not require new hardware. The Aido Rover test can follow once InGen finds a site with two recurring workflows. The humanoid test should stay focused on one task.

## Limitations

This memo uses public information available by July 17, 2026. Vendor-reported prices, deployment numbers, and performance figures come from company websites and press releases, and they are not independently audited. Prices also change quickly in this market, so the benchmarks should be rechecked before any pricing decision. The four tests above are meant to guide pilot design, not to replace technical validation or customer references.
